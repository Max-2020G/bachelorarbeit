\chapter{Introduction}
That light is, among other things, a wave and therefore occurs at different wavelengths has been
known in physics for a very long time. This spectrum of light is also called the electromagnetic
spectrum, or EM spectrum for short, which is shown in \autoref{fig:Spek}.
While a large part of this spectrum is easy to generate, significant problems arise at certain
frequency ranges.
This problematic region is the so-called THz gap, as shown in \autoref{fig:THz-lucke}.
It lies in the frequency range \SIrange{0.1}{10}{\tera\hertz} or the wavelength range
\SIrange{30}{3000}{\micro\meter}\cite{Williams_2006}.

    \label{fig:Spek}
\end{figure}

Frequencies below the THz range can be generated well using electronic oscillators.
However, as the frequency increases, more and more problems arise, for example due to
parasitic capacitances, which result in less and less power being emitted as radiation.
On the other hand, simple black-body sources or diode lasers can generate high power
in the range above the THz gap.
However, this power drops off sharply there as the frequency decreases.
This results in a gap in which it is very difficult to generate an electromagnetic wave at this
frequency.
THz radiation is interesting for several reasons: on the one hand, THz radiation, like X-rays, can
be used to detect tumors and other tissue abnormalities, without causing the genetic damage
associated with X-rays. On the other hand, it can also be used for material characterization in
physics without being destructive\,\cite{PAWAR2013157}.
Nowadays, this gap is bridged by, for example, photoconductive antennas
\cite{10.1117/1.OE.56.1.010901}, optical rectification, and THz quantum cascade lasers \cite{Dean_2014}.
This bachelor's thesis focuses in particular on optical rectification, since this is the main
mechanism by which the organic emitters generate THz radiation.
There have long been other crystals, such as ZnTe, that can generate THz radiation via
optical rectification; however, these have the problem that they can only be
manufactured by a few producers, who can therefore set the price as they see fit, and
the thickness of the crystals is also fixed, which in turn fixes the maximum power that
these crystals can withstand.
Therefore, this bachelor's thesis attempts to generate THz radiation using organic crystals
and to subsequently evaluate this THz radiation in various ways to determine whether these
crystals can replace ZnTe crystals.
These organic crystals can namely be grown as desired at the Faculty of Chemistry at TU Dortmund,
meaning that the thickness of the crystals can also be adjusted arbitrarily in the future, which
makes it possible to generate THz radiation with higher power.
In the following thesis, \autoref{sec:Theo} first explains the theoretical foundation for
generating THz radiation using optical rectification and the subsequent measurement using
electro-optic sampling.
Following this, \autoref{sec:auf} shows the setup of the time-delay setup using a pump-probe
system and explains its functionality.
Finally, \autoref{sec:aus} evaluates and discusses the measurement results.
