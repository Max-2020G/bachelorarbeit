\chapter{Experimental Method}
\label{sec:auf}
The laser underlying the setup is a pulsed Ti:sapphire laser, which is guided through an
amplifier and generates pulses in the kHz range.
To generate the THz radiation and subsequently measure it, a pump-probe technique is used in a
time-domain spectroscopy setup. The concept is based on the fact that there is a pump beam and a
probe beam from the laser, where the pump beam, which has a higher intensity, passes through the
generator crystal, while the probe beam, which has a lower intensity, does not pass through it.
The combined beams are then guided through an EOS setup and measured there.
The exact setup is explained in the following.

\section{Terahertz Time-Domain Spectroscopy}
At the beginning, the beam enters the setup with a power of $Platzhalter\,\unit{mW}$.
This can be seen in the top left of \autoref{fig:thz-aufbau}.
Afterwards, this beam hits a beam splitter, which transmits $99\%$ of the intensity; this then
becomes the pump beam, shown in green in the figure, which is described in more detail in
\autoref{sec:pump}.
The reflected part of the beam, which has $1\%$ of the power of the initial beam, is then the
probe beam, shown in orange in \autoref{fig:thz-aufbau}, which is described in more detail in
\autoref{sec:probe}.



\section{Pump Beam}
\label{sec:pump}
The pump beam first passes through a $\lambda/2$ waveplate, followed by a Glan-Thompson prism.
There, the $\lambda/2$ waveplate sets the polarization of the beam, and the Glan-Thompson prism
subsequently filters the beam to an arbitrary intensity.
This setup thus allows the intensity of the beam to be adjusted arbitrarily.
After this intensity adjustment, the pump beam is directed into the delay stage.
The delay stage is a pair of movable mirrors that can move forwards and backwards; this delay
stage has a travel range of $20\,\unit{\milli\meter}$.
\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{content/bilder/thz_puls.png}
    \caption{THz pulse together with the sampling pulse, which scans across the THz pulse as
    the delay stage is shifted\,\cite{KÃ¼hn2011Nonlinear}.}
    \label{fig:thz-puls}
\end{figure}

The delay stage is followed by a plano-convex lens with a focal length of $100\,\unit{\milli\meter}$.
This focal point is located $100\,\unit{\milli\meter}$ in front of the first subsequent parabolic
mirror, since this mirror also has a focal length of $100\,\unit{\milli\meter}$ and therefore
produces a collinear beam after the first parabolic mirror.
Between the first parabolic mirror and the lens is the generator crystal, which in the first step
is a ZnTe crystal used to determine a baseline value, and which is subsequently replaced by the
two different organic crystal samples in order to test their ability to generate THz radiation.
The optical crystals are placed on a mount that can be precisely adjusted in the x, y, and z
coordinates, so that the organic crystals can be positioned in such a way that they are fully
illuminated by the laser.
After this, the beam passes through a chopper. This interrupts the signal at a set frequency of
around $1500\,\unit{Hz}$, which is used for noise suppression, as described in more detail in
\autoref{sec:EOS}.
The laser then passes through 3 further parabolic mirrors.
Between the first and second parabolic mirrors, a silicon wafer is placed, which filters out the
remaining near-infrared light that hit the generator crystal and was not transmitted.
The 4th and final parabolic mirror focuses the beam onto the detection crystal, where a spatial
overlap with the probe beam must be created.
This means that the two focal points of the probe and pump THz beams lie at the same location;
this is achieved using a camera and by adjusting the last parabolic mirror of the pump THz beam
as well as adjusting the last regular mirror of the probe beam.


\section{Probe Beam}
\label{sec:probe}
At the beginning, the probe beam, like the pump beam before it, passes through a setup consisting
of a $\lambda/2$ waveplate followed by a Glan-Thompson prism; as with the pump beam, this is used
as an adjustable filter to precisely regulate the intensity of the beam.
This is followed by a delay section, so that the path lengths for the pump and probe beams are
equal.
The probe beam then hits a plano-convex lens with a focal length of $100\,\unit{\milli\meter}$,
whose focal point is at the ZnTe detection crystal.
After the lens, the beam is guided through a hole in the 4th parabolic mirror, where it meets the
pump beam together at the detection crystal.


\section{Electro-Optic Sampling}
\label{sec:EOS}
Electro-optic sampling begins with the arrival of the probe and pump beams at the ZnTe detection
crystal.
In this crystal, the refractive index changes due to the so-called Pockels effect
\cite{KÃ¼hn2011Nonlinear} %$\delta n \propto n^3 r E_\text{THz}(t)$%
; it is important here that the probe pulse is extremely short, in the fs range, compared to the
pump THz pulse in the ps range, and therefore always samples only a single moment of the THz
pulse, as shown in \autoref{fig:thz-puls}.
This shift is achieved, as already mentioned, via the delay stage.
The change in the refractive index means that, due to a specific rotation of the crystal, it
becomes birefringent, which means that the vertical and horizontal components of the beam are
refracted differently.
This birefringence produces a small phase delay, and the beam, which was previously linearly
polarized, becomes elliptically polarized.
This beam then hits the $\lambda/4$ plate, which further rotates this elliptically polarized beam
and turns it into a nearly circularly polarized beam. This behavior arises because the $\lambda/4$
plate delays one direction of polarization by a quarter of the wavelength, which results in a
circularly polarized beam for a perfectly linearly polarized beam. Since an elliptical beam is
present here, however, the beam after the $\lambda/4$ plate is not perfectly circular but only
very slightly elliptical.
This beam then hits a Wollaston prism, which spatially splits the beam into its vertical and
horizontal polarization components; if the beam were a perfectly circularly polarized beam, the
two resulting beams would be exactly identical, but due to the detector crystal there is now a
slightly elliptical polarization present.
As a result, a small intensity difference exists between the two beams.
These two beams are then measured and amplified by two photodiodes.
From these two signals, the difference, i.e. the actual THz signal, can then be calculated.
The signal is then fed into the lock-in amplifier, where it is demodulated at the set frequency of
the chopper.
This means that the signals with the same frequency as the chopper are amplified, while the
remaining signals are suppressed using a low-pass filter.
In addition, these signals with the same frequency are averaged over many periods to further
reduce the noise.
